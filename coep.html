Background

What is Skull Stripping?

Skull stripping, in the context of medical imaging and neuroimaging, is a process used to remove or separate the skull and other non-brain tissues from an image or scan of the brain. The primary goal of skull stripping is to isolate the brain tissue so that it can be analyzed, measured, or reconstructed accurately without interference from surrounding structures such as the skull, scalp, or soft tissues. This is a crucial step in various neuroimaging applications, including brain segmentation, volume measurement, and the creation of 3D brain models for research and medical diagnosis. Skull stripping algorithms and techniques are commonly used in MRI (Magnetic Resonance Imaging) and CT (Computed Tomography) scans of the brain to improve the accuracy of brain analysis.

The Gap

Deep Learning for Skull Stripping

Traditional manual methods, although considered the gold standard, are time-consuming and impractical for large datasets. Automated techniques have emerged to address these challenges, with deep learning methods, particularly U-Net architectures, showing great promise. These methods offer robust and efficient solutions, reducing the need for manual intervention and facilitating seamless integration into neuroimaging pipelines. The ability of deep learning models to learn and generalize complex patterns makes them ideal for the intricate task of skull stripping.

Getting There

Methodology Used

The study compares three different 2D U-Net architectures: Vanilla, Residual, and Dense, using a well-curated dataset and robust pre-processing techniques.

The dataset used for this research is the Neurofeedback Skull-stripped (NFBS) repository, which contains 125 T1-weighted MRI scans with corresponding brain masks. Out of these, 110 scans were used for training and 15 for testing. The pre-processing stage involved Z-normalization to standardize the intensity values of the MRI scans. Additionally, data augmentation techniques, including spatial and intensity transformations, were applied to expand the training dataset fivefold. This augmentation aimed to enhance the model's ability to generalize across varied scanning conditions.

Three neural network architectures were evaluated for this study. The Vanilla 2D U-Net architecture employs basic 2D convolutions in its contractive and expansive paths, using ReLU activation and max-pooling layers. It consists of approximately 7.76 million trainable parameters. The Residual 2D U-Net architecture incorporates residual connections to improve information flow and feature reuse, resulting in about 9.90 million trainable parameters. The novel Dense 2D U-Net architecture leverages dense connections to facilitate extensive feature reuse, boasting 15.48 million trainable parameters. This model builds on the principles of Dense Convolutional Networks, offering enhanced feature aggregation across semantic scales.

The implementation of these models was carried out using TensorFlow with Keras APIs. The models were trained on an NVIDIA A100 GPU using the Adam optimizer with specific hyperparameters. The train/validation split was set to 90/10, with early stopping employed to prevent overfitting.

Got It

Results

The Dense 2D U-Net architecture outperformed both the Vanilla and Residual variants in terms of accuracy. The Vanilla 2D U-Net achieved a testing accuracy of 99.73% after 10 epochs. The Residual 2D U-Net achieved a testing accuracy of 99.72% after 9 epochs. The Dense 2D U-Net achieved the highest testing accuracy of 99.75% after 9 epochs. The Dense architecture's superior performance is attributed to its extensive feature reuse and improved aggregation capabilities, allowing it to achieve higher accuracy with fewer epochs.

Wrapping Up

Conclusion

This study demonstrates the efficacy of deep learning, specifically U-Net architectures, for the skull stripping task in neuroimaging. The Dense 2D U-Net architecture, in particular, shows significant promise due to its advanced feature reuse mechanisms. These findings can be extended to other neuroimaging tasks requiring skull stripping, offering a robust foundation for further research and development in automated neuroimaging workflows. Future work could explore additional augmentation techniques, deeper network architectures, and refined hyperparameter tuning to further enhance performance.
