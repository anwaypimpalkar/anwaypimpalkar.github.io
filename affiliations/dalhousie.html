Background

Wild Blueberries!

Wild blueberries, also known as Vaccinium angustifolium, grow low to the ground in rocky areas, offering a bold, untamed flavor that distinguishes them from their cultivated counterparts. These berries are not only packed with antioxidants and nutrients, making them a powerhouse of goodness, but also hold a unique place in North America's natural history, being one of the three berries native to the continent.

A Natural Growth Process

Unlike cultivated blueberries (Vaccinium corymbosum), which require careful planning and planting, wild blueberries grow naturally in fields and rocky hills called barrens. They have thrived without human intervention for thousands of years, making them a low-maintenance crop. Field owners usually take a hands-off approach throughout most of the growing season, only introducing bees to naturally pollinate the bushes. With a two-year crop cycle, these fields are pruned every other year using rotary mowers.

Distinctive Characteristics

Wild blueberries are much smaller than their cultivated relatives and vary in color from different shades of blue to almost black. Their taste ranges from very sweet to not-so-sweet, reflecting their genetic diversity. In contrast, cultivated blueberries are uniform in size, color, and taste due to selective breeding and farming practices. This genetic diversity, untouched by human intervention, makes wild blueberries a highly diverse crop compared to the uniformity of cultivated varieties.

Growth and Harvesting

Wild blueberry bushes, known as "lowbush," spread low and wide through runners, covering fields in a random manner. In contrast, "highbush" cultivated blueberries stand in straight rows and tower over the average person. Due to their low height and the often rocky terrain where they grow, many wild blueberry fields cannot be harvested with traditional machinery. Instead, they are often hand-harvested using rakes.

Harvesting Them

Mechanical Harvesting Methods

Harvesting wild blueberries by hand is a tiring and laborious task. To ease this process, advances have been made to use mechanical tractor-based harvesters. Using large harvesters for such small berries requires meticulous adjustments to the harvester head's height, speed, angle, and various other controls to ensure optimal performance. This careful calibration ensures that the berries are not crushed, missed during harvesting, or contaminated with soil. After meticulous monitoring, the berries are harvested and collected into large yellow bins at the back of the tractor, then shipped off for processing and sale.

Problems with Mechanization

The driver plays a crucial role, overseeing the all minor adjustments of the harvester head, monitoring the berries harvested, and determining when bins are full, and replacing them as needed. All of this is besides driving the tractor in the first place! The multitude of controls can get overwhelming for the operator very quickly. To address this challenge, our goal was to introduce automation to some of these tasks. We aimed to automate the measurement of berry volume in the bins and the automatic switching of bins once they are full. This advancement is the first step towards alleviating the burden on the driver while also enhancing the efficiency and precision of the harvesting process.

Solution

Sensing Methodology Overview

To accurately measure the volume of berries in the bins, we implemented a camera-based system. A Time-of-Flight and RGB camera are mounted on the harvester, facing downwards towards the bin. This setup utilizes image segmentation and point cloud processing algorithms to estimate the volume of the berries effectively. The system is designed to be robust, ensuring reliable performance in various lighting conditions, including harsh sunlight. It also adapts to different bin depths and colors. This significantly enhances the efficiency of the harvesting process by providing precise volume measurements and reducing the manual workload for the operator. Details of these algorithms can be provided upon request.
